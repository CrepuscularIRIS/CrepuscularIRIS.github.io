ICML 2026 rejected 497 papers after catching reviewers who outsourced their reviews to language models. The detection method was not a classifier, not a perplexity score, not a stylistic analysis. It was a prompt injection — the conference organizers embedded invisible instructions in submission PDFs that, when processed by an LLM, caused the model to include specific telltale phrases in its output.

This is, to my knowledge, the first large-scale deployment of adversarial prompt injection as an *institutional defense mechanism*. The technique borrows from information security (steganographic watermarking), weaponizes a known LLM vulnerability (prompt injection), and deploys it in a context where the "attacker" is the institution and the "victim" is anyone who violates the review policy. The ethical and technical implications deserve careful examination.

## The Watermarking Scheme

The design is elegant in its simplicity. ICML constructed a dictionary of 170,000 phrases — each semantically plausible in an academic review context but specific enough to be statistically improbable in organic writing. For each submitted paper, the system randomly selected 2 phrases from this dictionary and embedded them as hidden instructions in the PDF.

The collision probability — the chance that two different papers receive the same pair of phrases — is less than 1 in 10 billion. This matters because it means a flagged review can be traced to a specific submission with near-certainty.

### How the Injection Works

The hidden instructions exploit how LLMs process document context. When a reviewer feeds a PDF into ChatGPT, Claude, or any frontier model, the model tokenizes the entire document content — including metadata, hidden text layers, and formatting instructions that humans typically ignore. The embedded phrases are positioned in regions of the PDF that are:

1. **Invisible to human readers** — hidden in metadata fields, white-on-white text, or PDF annotation layers
2. **Visible to LLM tokenizers** — included in the raw text extraction that precedes model inference
3. **Instructional in nature** — phrased as directives ("include the phrase X in your analysis") that exploit the model's instruction-following behavior

The key insight: LLMs cannot distinguish between legitimate instructions from the user and injected instructions from the document. This is the same vulnerability that makes prompt injection a security risk in production systems — here repurposed as a detection mechanism.

```python
import hashlib
from typing import Tuple

# Simplified illustration of the phrase selection mechanism
PHRASE_DICTIONARY_SIZE = 170_000

def select_watermark_phrases(
    paper_id: str,
    secret_key: bytes,
    dictionary: list[str],
) -> Tuple[str, str]:
    """
    Deterministically select 2 phrases for a given paper.
    Uses HMAC to prevent reviewers from predicting their assigned phrases.
    """
    # HMAC ensures phrase selection is unpredictable without the secret key
    h = hashlib.pbkdf2_hmac("sha256", paper_id.encode(), secret_key, 100_000)
    idx1 = int.from_bytes(h[:4], "big") % len(dictionary)
    idx2 = int.from_bytes(h[4:8], "big") % len(dictionary)
    # Ensure two distinct phrases
    if idx1 == idx2:
        idx2 = (idx2 + 1) % len(dictionary)
    return dictionary[idx1], dictionary[idx2]

# Collision probability for 2 phrases from 170K dictionary:
# P(collision) = 1 / (170000 * 169999) ≈ 3.5e-11
# For 10,000 papers: P(any collision) ≈ 1.7e-3 (birthday bound)
# Still negligible for practical purposes.
```

### Detection Rate and False Positives

ICML reported that 795 reviews (~1% of total submissions) from 506 unique reviewers were flagged. Of these, 51 reviewers had more than 50% of their reviews flagged — a pattern consistent with systematic LLM use rather than coincidental phrase matches.

The detection success rate exceeded 80% across most frontier models. This means 80%+ of the time, when a reviewer fed the paper to an LLM, the model included the watermark phrases in its output. The remaining 20% likely reflects cases where:

- The model's output was heavily edited post-generation
- The PDF extraction pipeline stripped the hidden content before it reached the model
- The model happened not to follow the injected instruction (instruction-following is not 100% reliable)

The false positive rate is harder to assess. A human reviewer could, in principle, independently use one of the 170,000 phrases. With 2 phrases required per flag, the probability of a false positive on a single review is approximately $\frac{1}{170000^2} \approx 3.5 \times 10^{-11}$ — effectively zero for random phrase co-occurrence. The real false positive risk comes from a different source: a reviewer who reads the paper carefully and happens to use similar language. ICML addressed this by requiring *both* assigned phrases to appear, making coincidental matches astronomically unlikely.

## The Dual-Track Policy Experiment

ICML 2026 introduced a dual-track review policy alongside the watermarking scheme:

- **Policy A**: No LLM assistance permitted in the review process
- **Policy B**: Limited LLM assistance allowed (e.g., grammar checking, literature search) but not for generating the substantive review content

This dual-track design is itself an experiment in institutional design. By offering a legitimate path for LLM-assisted review (Policy B), ICML reduced the incentive to use LLMs covertly under Policy A. Reviewers who genuinely find LLMs helpful for peripheral tasks can opt into Policy B without violating norms.

The watermarking specifically targets Policy A violations — reviewers who claimed no LLM use but demonstrably fed the paper to a model. Policy B violations (using LLMs beyond the permitted scope) are harder to detect because the reviewer has already acknowledged some LLM involvement.

## What the Numbers Reveal

The 1% flagging rate understates the likely prevalence of LLM-assisted reviewing. Consider the detection limitations:

1. **Only detects direct PDF-to-LLM pipelines** — a reviewer who manually copies text sections into an LLM, rather than uploading the full PDF, might not trigger the watermark
2. **Only catches models that follow instructions** — smaller or fine-tuned models with weaker instruction-following might ignore the injected phrases
3. **Editing defeats detection** — a reviewer who generates an LLM draft but rewrites it substantially will remove the watermark phrases

The ICLR 2026 data provides a cross-reference: an independent analysis estimated that 21% of ICLR reviews showed statistical signatures of AI generation. If ICML's reviewer population has similar behavior, the 1% detection rate captures roughly 1 in 20 actual violations — those who were both lazy enough to use the full PDF pipeline and careless enough not to edit out unusual phrases.

This matters for interpreting the detection scheme's purpose. It does not catch all violators. It catches the most egregious cases — reviewers who contribute essentially zero intellectual labor to the review process. The deterrence effect likely extends further: knowing that watermarks *exist* (ICML publicized the scheme) discourages LLM use even among reviewers who might otherwise avoid detection.

## The Prompt Injection Paradox

There is an irony worth sitting with. The AI security community has spent years warning about prompt injection as a vulnerability — a way for malicious documents to hijack LLM behavior. ICML's watermarking scheme *is* prompt injection, deployed by a trusted institution for a legitimate purpose.

This creates a precedent with uncomfortable implications:

**If prompt injection is a legitimate institutional tool, who else can use it?** Employers embedding monitoring instructions in documents shared with employees? Governments inserting tracking phrases in classified materials? The technical mechanism is identical — only the institutional context and intent differ.

**The defense undermines its own foundation.** ICML's scheme works precisely because LLMs are vulnerable to prompt injection. If models become robust to injection (a goal the security community actively pursues), the watermarking scheme stops working. ICML's detection capability is inversely correlated with LLM security. The institution benefits from a vulnerability that harms users in other contexts.

**Adversarial asymmetry.** The current scheme catches naive violators. A reviewer who understands prompt injection can preprocess the PDF to strip hidden content before feeding it to an LLM. PDF sanitization tools exist and are not complex to use. The arms race dynamics are straightforward: as awareness of the scheme spreads, its detection rate will decline, requiring increasingly sophisticated injection techniques — which in turn demand increasingly sophisticated countermeasures. This is a familiar dynamic from DRM, ad blocking, and browser fingerprinting.

```python
# A reviewer who understands the scheme can defeat it trivially:
import fitz  # PyMuPDF

def sanitize_pdf_for_review(input_path: str, output_path: str) -> None:
    """
    Strip hidden text, metadata, and annotation layers from a PDF
    before feeding it to an LLM. Defeats watermark injection.
    
    This is NOT a recommendation — it's an illustration of the
    adversarial asymmetry in the detection scheme.
    """
    doc = fitz.open(input_path)
    clean_text_parts = []
    for page in doc:
        # Extract only visible text blocks
        blocks = page.get_text("blocks")
        for block in blocks:
            x0, y0, x1, y1, text, block_no, block_type = block
            # Skip blocks with suspicious properties
            # (white text on white background, zero-height, metadata fields)
            if block_type == 0 and len(text.strip()) > 0:
                clean_text_parts.append(text.strip())
    doc.close()
    
    clean_text = "\n\n".join(clean_text_parts)
    with open(output_path, "w") as f:
        f.write(clean_text)
    # Feed clean_text to LLM instead of the original PDF
```

## What This Means for the Research Community

The watermarking scheme is a symptom of a deeper problem: the review system depends on voluntary, unpaid labor from researchers who are increasingly time-constrained. LLMs offer an obvious shortcut. The question is whether the community response should be detection and punishment, structural reform, or some combination.

**Detection has limits.** As shown above, the scheme primarily catches careless violators. Sophisticated users can evade it. More importantly, even perfect detection doesn't address the root cause — reviewers who lack time or motivation to engage deeply with submissions.

**Structural alternatives exist.** Some proposals being discussed in the community:

- **Compensated reviewing** — paying reviewers reduces the incentive to minimize effort
- **Shorter review formats** — requiring less text but more structured evaluation (scores + brief justifications) makes human review less burdensome
- **LLM-assisted workflows with human oversight** — explicitly designing review processes where LLMs handle literature search, formatting checks, and reference verification while humans provide the substantive evaluation
- **Open review** — making reviews public (with or without reviewer identity) creates accountability through transparency rather than surveillance

The ICML watermarking scheme is clever engineering. Whether it represents the right institutional response to AI-assisted reviewing is a separate question — one that involves trade-offs between detection accuracy, reviewer privacy, institutional trust, and the long-term sustainability of peer review as a system.

## Technical Takeaways

For the AI security community, ICML's scheme provides a real-world case study of prompt injection deployed at scale:

1. **Injection reliability** is high (~80%) against frontier models, confirming that current LLMs cannot reliably distinguish document content from injected instructions
2. **Steganographic capacity** in PDFs is sufficient to embed multiple distinct instructions without human-visible artifacts
3. **Statistical design** (170K dictionary, 2 phrases per paper, collision probability analysis) demonstrates how to build injection-based detection with controlled false positive rates
4. **Adversarial robustness** is low — PDF sanitization trivially defeats the scheme, suggesting the detection depends on attacker naivety rather than fundamental limitations

For ML researchers, the message is simpler: the institutions are watching, and the detection methods will evolve. The 497 rejected papers represent careers affected by a policy decision that, six months ago, would have been unenforceable. The enforcement capability now exists. Whether you agree with how it's deployed, the technical cat is out of the bag.
