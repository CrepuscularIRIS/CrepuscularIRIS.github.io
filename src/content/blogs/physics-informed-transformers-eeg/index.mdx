Most EEG foundation models treat electrode signals as generic time series. They tokenize voltage traces, feed them through a standard Transformer, and hope the self-attention mechanism learns the relevant signal structure from data alone. This works — to a point. Models like EEG-GPT, LaBraM, and NeuroGPT achieve respectable decoding accuracy on motor imagery, emotion recognition, and event-related potential detection when given enough training data from the target domain.

The problem is zero-shot transfer. When you train an EEG foundation model on one dataset (say, motor imagery from BCI Competition IV) and deploy it on another (say, visual imagery from the THINGS-EEG dataset), performance drops sharply. The model has learned the statistical regularities of its training distribution, but it hasn't learned the physics that generate those regularities. The statistical patterns are dataset-specific. The physics are universal.

DeeperBrain, updated in April 2026, takes the opposite approach: integrate the biophysics of EEG generation directly into the Transformer architecture. The result is a model that outperforms black-box models 100x its size on zero-shot decoding tasks — because it knows, architecturally, things about EEG that data-driven models have to discover from scratch.

## What EEG Actually Is (Physically)

Before explaining the architecture, it's worth being precise about what an EEG electrode measures — because this determines what inductive biases are correct.

An EEG electrode on the scalp does not measure the activity of the neurons directly beneath it. It measures the superposition of electric fields generated by millions of synchronously active cortical pyramidal neurons, after those fields have propagated through cerebrospinal fluid, skull bone, and skin. This propagation process — **volume conduction** — acts as a spatial low-pass filter that smears source activity across multiple electrodes with distance-dependent attenuation.

The consequence: adjacent electrodes are highly correlated not because they measure similar neural activity, but because they measure the same neural activity through slightly different propagation paths. A standard Transformer's self-attention mechanism treats inter-electrode correlations as data-driven relationships to learn. But these correlations have a known physical origin — the geometry of the head and the conductivity of tissue layers. Encoding this physics removes something the model would otherwise spend capacity learning.

The second physical constraint is **neurodynamic oscillations**. Neural populations don't fire randomly — they oscillate in characteristic frequency bands (delta: 1-4 Hz, theta: 4-8 Hz, alpha: 8-13 Hz, beta: 13-30 Hz, gamma: 30-100+ Hz) that reflect distinct computational modes of the underlying cortex. These oscillations undergo **phase-resetting** in response to stimuli: an external event (a visual flash, a motor command, an auditory tone) causes the ongoing oscillation to reset its phase, aligning neural population timing to the stimulus.

Phase-resetting is the primary mechanism by which event-related potentials (ERPs) are generated. A standard Transformer processes the raw voltage trace without knowing that the relevant signal is a phase perturbation of an ongoing oscillation. It has to learn both the oscillation and the perturbation from data. DeeperBrain separates them architecturally.

## Volume Conduction-Aware Encoding

The first architectural component is a spatial encoder that replaces the standard positional embedding with a physics-informed electrode relationship model.

In a standard EEG Transformer, each electrode gets a learned positional embedding (or a sinusoidal one based on channel index). This tells the model that channel 1 is "different from" channel 2, but not *how* — the relationship is entirely learned from data.

DeeperBrain replaces this with a **volume conduction kernel** that computes the expected signal mixing between any pair of electrodes based on their 3D positions and a simplified head model:

```python
import torch
import torch.nn as nn
import numpy as np
from dataclasses import dataclass


@dataclass(frozen=True)
class HeadModelConfig:
    """Simplified 3-sphere head model parameters."""
    scalp_radius: float = 0.092      # meters
    skull_radius: float = 0.086
    brain_radius: float = 0.080
    scalp_conductivity: float = 0.33  # S/m
    skull_conductivity: float = 0.0042
    brain_conductivity: float = 0.33


class VolumeConductionEncoder(nn.Module):
    """Physics-informed spatial encoding for EEG electrodes."""

    def __init__(
        self,
        num_electrodes: int,
        embed_dim: int,
        electrode_positions: torch.Tensor,  # (num_electrodes, 3) in meters
        head_cfg: HeadModelConfig = HeadModelConfig(),
    ):
        super().__init__()
        self.embed_dim = embed_dim

        # Compute pairwise lead field attenuation matrix
        # Simplified exponential decay model (not a full 3-sphere model)
        distances = torch.cdist(electrode_positions, electrode_positions)
        conductivity_ratio = (
            head_cfg.skull_conductivity / head_cfg.scalp_conductivity
        )

        # Simplified attenuation: exponential decay with skull resistance
        attenuation = torch.exp(
            -distances / (head_cfg.skull_radius * conductivity_ratio)
        )
        self.register_buffer("attenuation_matrix", attenuation)

        # Project physics-informed relationships to embedding space
        self.spatial_projection = nn.Linear(num_electrodes, embed_dim)
        self.channel_embedding = nn.Embedding(num_electrodes, embed_dim)

    def forward(self, x: torch.Tensor) -> torch.Tensor:
        """
        Args:
            x: Raw EEG signals (batch, num_electrodes, time_steps)
        Returns:
            Spatially encoded signals (batch, num_electrodes, embed_dim)
        """
        batch_size, num_ch, time_steps = x.shape

        # Apply volume conduction mixing — this is the physics
        # Each electrode's representation includes its expected
        # contribution from all other electrodes
        vc_features = torch.matmul(
            self.attenuation_matrix, x
        )  # (batch, num_ch, time_steps)

        # Project to embedding space
        spatial_embed = self.spatial_projection(
            vc_features.transpose(1, 2)
        ).transpose(1, 2)  # (batch, num_ch, embed_dim)

        # Add learned channel identity
        channel_ids = torch.arange(num_ch, device=x.device)
        channel_embed = self.channel_embedding(channel_ids)

        return spatial_embed + channel_embed.unsqueeze(0)
```

The attenuation matrix encodes the physical fact that electrode Fp1 and Fp2 (both on the forehead) share more signal than Fp1 and O1 (forehead and occipital pole) — not because a neural network learned this correlation from data, but because physics says so. The model still has learned parameters (the spatial projection and channel embedding), but these parameters operate on top of the physical prior rather than having to rediscover it.

## Neurodynamics-Aware Temporal Gates

The second component addresses the temporal structure of EEG. Standard Transformers use self-attention over time steps, which is effective for modeling long-range dependencies but agnostic to the oscillatory structure of neural signals.

DeeperBrain introduces **temporal gates** that decompose the input signal into frequency bands and detect phase-resetting events within each band:

```python
class NeurodynamicTemporalGate(nn.Module):
    """Detect phase-resetting events in neural oscillations."""

    # Canonical EEG frequency bands (Hz)
    BANDS: dict[str, tuple[float, float]] = {
        "delta": (1.0, 4.0),
        "theta": (4.0, 8.0),
        "alpha": (8.0, 13.0),
        "beta": (13.0, 30.0),
        "gamma": (30.0, 100.0),
    }

    def __init__(self, embed_dim: int, sample_rate: float = 256.0):
        super().__init__()
        self.sample_rate = sample_rate
        self.num_bands = len(self.BANDS)

        # Learnable bandpass filters initialized at canonical frequencies
        self.filter_centers = nn.Parameter(
            torch.tensor([
                (low + high) / 2 for low, high in self.BANDS.values()
            ])
        )
        self.filter_widths = nn.Parameter(
            torch.tensor([
                (high - low) / 2 for low, high in self.BANDS.values()
            ])
        )

        # Phase-reset detector: identifies sudden phase discontinuities
        self.phase_detector = nn.Conv1d(
            in_channels=self.num_bands,
            out_channels=self.num_bands,
            kernel_size=5,
            padding=2,
            groups=self.num_bands,  # Depthwise — each band independent
        )

        # Gate projection
        self.gate_projection = nn.Linear(self.num_bands, embed_dim)

    def _bandpass(
        self, x: torch.Tensor, center: torch.Tensor, width: torch.Tensor,
    ) -> torch.Tensor:
        """Differentiable bandpass via frequency-domain Gaussian window."""
        seq_len = x.size(-1)
        freqs = torch.fft.rfftfreq(seq_len, d=1.0 / self.sample_rate)
        freqs = freqs.to(x.device)

        # Gaussian bandpass window
        window = torch.exp(
            -0.5 * ((freqs - center) / width) ** 2
        )

        x_fft = torch.fft.rfft(x, dim=-1)
        filtered = torch.fft.irfft(x_fft * window, n=seq_len, dim=-1)
        return filtered

    def forward(self, x: torch.Tensor) -> torch.Tensor:
        """
        Args:
            x: Time-domain signal (batch, channels, time_steps)
        Returns:
            Temporal gate signal (batch, channels, embed_dim)
        """
        batch_size, num_ch, time_steps = x.shape

        # Decompose into frequency bands
        band_signals = []
        for i in range(self.num_bands):
            filtered = self._bandpass(
                x, self.filter_centers[i], self.filter_widths[i],
            )
            # Compute instantaneous phase via Hilbert transform
            analytic = torch.view_as_complex(
                torch.stack([filtered, self._hilbert_imag(filtered)], dim=-1)
            )
            phase = torch.angle(analytic)
            # Phase derivative — spikes at phase-reset events
            phase_diff = torch.diff(phase, dim=-1, prepend=phase[..., :1])
            band_signals.append(phase_diff)

        # Stack bands and detect phase resets
        bands = torch.stack(band_signals, dim=2)  # (batch, ch, bands, time)
        bands_flat = bands.view(-1, self.num_bands, time_steps)
        reset_signals = torch.sigmoid(self.phase_detector(bands_flat))

        # Pool over time to get per-channel gate
        gate = reset_signals.mean(dim=-1)  # (batch*ch, bands)
        gate = gate.view(batch_size, num_ch, self.num_bands)

        return self.gate_projection(gate)  # (batch, ch, embed_dim)

    @staticmethod
    def _hilbert_imag(x: torch.Tensor) -> torch.Tensor:
        """Compute imaginary part of analytic signal via FFT.

        Uses the standard frequency-domain method: zero out negative
        frequencies, double positive frequencies, then take the
        imaginary part of the inverse FFT.
        """
        n = x.size(-1)
        x_fft = torch.fft.fft(x, dim=-1)
        # Build the frequency-domain multiplier h
        h = torch.zeros(n, device=x.device)
        h[0] = 1  # DC component unchanged
        if n % 2 == 0:
            h[n // 2] = 1  # Nyquist unchanged
            h[1:n // 2] = 2  # Double positive frequencies
        else:
            h[1:(n + 1) // 2] = 2
        analytic_fft = x_fft * h
        return torch.fft.ifft(analytic_fft, dim=-1).imag
```

The key design choice: the filter centers and widths are *initialized* at canonical EEG frequencies but are *learnable parameters*. During training, the model can adjust the frequency decomposition to better match the actual oscillatory structure of the data — which may differ from textbook values depending on the subject, recording hardware, and task. The physics provides initialization; the data provides refinement.

The phase-reset detector uses depthwise convolution (each band processed independently) to identify moments where the instantaneous phase of an oscillation changes abruptly. These moments correspond to neural events — stimulus onsets, motor commands, attentional shifts — that are the primary carriers of decodable information in EEG.

## Why This Outperforms Larger Models

The performance advantage of physics-informed architectures on zero-shot transfer has a precise explanation.

A standard Transformer trained on Dataset A learns, among other things, the volume conduction pattern of the specific electrode cap used in Dataset A, the specific frequency structure of the subjects in Dataset A, and the specific noise characteristics of the amplifier used to record Dataset A. These are all confounded with the neural signals of interest.

When transferred to Dataset B — different cap, different subjects, different amplifier — everything the model learned about the recording setup is wrong. Only what it learned about the underlying neural signals transfers. But in a standard model, these are entangled in the same parameters.

DeeperBrain separates them architecturally:

- Volume conduction is handled by the spatial encoder, which takes electrode positions as input. Different caps → different positions → different attenuation matrices. The physics adapts automatically.
- Oscillatory structure is handled by the temporal gates, which are initialized at universal frequency bands. The learned adjustments are small (refinements to center frequencies and bandwidths), so they transfer more robustly than parameters that must learn the entire frequency structure from scratch.
- What remains for the Transformer to learn is the neural signal itself — the relationship between brain states and cognitive events — which is the part that actually generalizes across datasets.

This is why a 300M parameter DeeperBrain outperforms 30B parameter black-box models on zero-shot transfer: the physics-informed components handle the dataset-specific variation that the black-box model wastes parameters learning and then can't transfer.

## The Broader Principle: Inductive Bias as Compression

DeeperBrain is a specific instance of a general principle: known physics is free compression. When you know the generating process of your data, encoding that process into the architecture reduces the hypothesis space the model must search. Fewer hypotheses means less data needed, faster convergence, and better generalization.

This principle appears across ML:

- **Convolutional neural networks** encode translation equivariance — the fact that a cat is a cat regardless of where it appears in the image. This is "physics" in the sense of image formation geometry.
- **Graph neural networks** encode relational structure — the fact that molecular properties depend on bond topology, not on how you happened to number the atoms.
- **Equivariant neural networks** encode symmetry groups — rotational and translational symmetries in physical systems.

DeeperBrain adds two more:
- **Volume conduction encoding** — the physics of electromagnetic propagation through tissue.
- **Oscillatory phase encoding** — the neurodynamics of cortical population activity.

The pattern is always the same: if you know a constraint that the true generating function satisfies, build it into the architecture rather than hoping the model learns it from data. The model will learn it eventually — but "eventually" means "with enough data and parameters," and on zero-shot transfer, you have neither.

## Practical Implications for BCI Research

For researchers building EEG decoding systems, DeeperBrain's architecture suggests several immediate design choices:

**Always use electrode positions.** If your model doesn't know where the electrodes are on the head, it can't account for volume conduction. Most current models treat electrode channels as interchangeable features with learned positional embeddings. This wastes model capacity on something you already know.

**Decompose before attending.** Standard Transformers apply self-attention to the raw signal. Decomposing into frequency bands first — even with fixed (non-learned) bandpass filters — gives the attention mechanism cleaner features to work with. The attention can then learn cross-frequency interactions (like theta-gamma coupling) without first having to learn the frequency decomposition itself.

**Initialize from physics, optimize from data.** The strongest configuration in the DeeperBrain ablations uses learnable parameters initialized at physical values. Fixed-physics models underperform because the true generating process deviates from the simplified head model. Fully learned models underperform because they can't find the right configuration from random initialization without large datasets. The sweet spot is physics-informed initialization with data-driven refinement.

**Evaluate on zero-shot transfer, not within-dataset accuracy.** Within-dataset accuracy measures memorization of dataset-specific statistical patterns. Zero-shot transfer measures understanding of the underlying neural signals. For clinical BCI applications — where the system must work on new patients without retraining — zero-shot transfer is the metric that matters.

## What Comes Next

The DeeperBrain approach points toward a research direction that the BCI community has been slow to adopt: **neurophysiology-aware deep learning**. Most progress in EEG decoding over the past five years has come from scaling standard architectures (bigger Transformers, more data, longer training). DeeperBrain shows that architectural innovation — specifically, encoding known neuroscience into the model structure — can achieve more with less.

The open questions are about how far this principle extends. Volume conduction and oscillatory dynamics are well-characterized physics with mature mathematical models. Other aspects of neural signal generation — cortical folding effects on dipole orientation, individual differences in skull conductivity, dynamic changes in brain state during recording — are less well-modeled but equally important for decoding accuracy. Each additional physical constraint that can be reliably encoded into the architecture reduces the data requirements for zero-shot transfer.

The ultimate goal for EEG foundation models is a system that works on any subject, any electrode configuration, any recording setup, on first contact — the way a radiologist reads an X-ray from any machine. DeeperBrain doesn't achieve this, but it demonstrates that physics-informed architectures move in the right direction faster than parameter scaling alone.
