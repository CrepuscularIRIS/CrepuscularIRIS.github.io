Speech BCIs have followed a fixed pipeline for years: record neural activity, decode phonemes, feed phonemes into a language model, output text. Each stage is optimized independently. The language model compensates for the phoneme decoder's errors, but it cannot propagate gradient information back to improve the decoder. This cascaded architecture works — the BrainGate team and others have built impressive real-time systems with it — but it places a hard ceiling on performance because errors at each stage compound rather than cancel.

BIT (BraIn-to-Text), presented at ICLR 2026 by Zhang et al., breaks this pipeline open. The paper introduces two things that matter: a neural encoder pretrained across species and tasks using self-supervised learning, and an end-to-end architecture that connects this encoder directly to an audio LLM for text generation. The result is a 58% reduction in word error rate over the previous best end-to-end method, dropping from 24.69% to 10.22%.

## The Cascaded Ceiling

To understand why end-to-end matters, look at what the standard pipeline actually does.

A typical speech BCI records from a Utah microelectrode array implanted in the ventral premotor and precentral gyrus. The recording captures two signal types: thresholded spike counts (single-unit activity) and spike-band power (multi-unit population activity). These signals get binned into 20ms windows, producing a matrix of neural features over time.

The cascaded decoder processes this matrix through three independent stages:

```
Neural activity → [Phoneme Decoder] → phoneme sequence
                                           ↓
                        [n-gram Language Model] → candidate sentences
                                           ↓
                              [Rescoring/Beam Search] → final text
```

Each arrow is a hard boundary. The phoneme decoder is trained to minimize phoneme classification error. The language model is trained on text corpora. Neither can influence the other's training. If the phoneme decoder consistently confuses /b/ and /p/, the language model learns to work around this confusion — but the phoneme decoder never receives the signal that distinguishing these phonemes matters more for sentence-level accuracy than distinguishing /s/ and /z/.

The best cascaded systems achieve around 6-9% WER on attempted speech, which is remarkable. But end-to-end training can do better because it lets the encoder learn representations that are optimized for the final objective (correct text) rather than an intermediate proxy (correct phonemes).

## Cross-Species Pretraining

BIT's first contribution is a pretrained neural encoder. This follows the now-standard recipe in deep learning: pretrain on a large, diverse dataset with self-supervised learning, then fine-tune on the target task.

The twist is that the pretraining data comes from both humans and monkeys.

The encoder is a transformer that processes neural recordings in time patches. Each patch covers multiple 20ms bins and captures the temporal dynamics of neural firing patterns. The pretraining objective is masked patch modeling — mask out random time patches and train the encoder to reconstruct them from the surrounding context.

The pretraining dataset: 367 hours of Utah array recordings spanning:

- **Human speech data** — attempted speech recordings from paralyzed participants producing sentences
- **Human motor data** — arm and hand movement recordings
- **Monkey motor data** — reaching and grasping task recordings from rhesus macaques

Two design choices stand out.

**Cross-species transfer works.** Monkey motor cortex data improves human speech decoding. This isn't obvious — monkey cortex has different laminar organization, different electrode placement relative to the cortical surface, and the tasks are entirely different (arm movements vs. speech production). But the pretraining objective forces the encoder to learn general principles of neural population dynamics: how populations of neurons coordinate activity over time, how neural trajectories unfold during motor planning and execution. These dynamics are conserved across species because motor cortex shares evolutionary structure.

The ablation is clean:

```
Encoder Configuration      | WER (cascaded) | Improvement
BIT-TFS (trained from scratch) | baseline       | —
BIT-Human (human pretraining)  | better         | cross-task helps
BIT-All (human + monkey)       | best           | cross-species helps
```

**Subject-specific adapters handle variability.** Each recording subject gets a small linear projection layer that maps their electrode configuration into the shared encoder space. This handles the fact that electrode placement, impedance, and signal quality vary dramatically between subjects and implant sessions. The shared encoder learns universal neural dynamics; the subject-specific layers handle the measurement noise.

## End-to-End Architecture

The second contribution connects the pretrained neural encoder to an audio LLM for direct sentence generation.

The analogy the authors use is LLaVA: where LLaVA gives a language model "eyes" by connecting an image encoder to an LLM, BIT gives a language model a "brain" by connecting the neural encoder to an audio-capable LLM.

The specific model: Aero1-Audio 1.5B, an audio-extended version of Qwen2.5-1.5B. The architecture:

```
Neural activity → [Pretrained BIT Encoder] → neural embeddings
                                                    ↓
                                        [Contrastive Alignment]
                                                    ↓
                    Audio LLM (Aero1-Audio 1.5B) → text output
```

The contrastive alignment step is critical. Neural embeddings live in a different representational space than audio embeddings. The alignment training uses contrastive learning to map neural embeddings close to the audio embeddings of the corresponding speech, creating a shared space where the LLM can process neural input as if it were degraded audio.

The end-to-end system trains with a combined loss: contrastive loss for alignment plus language modeling loss for text generation. Gradients flow from the text output all the way back through the alignment layer into the neural encoder. This means the encoder learns to produce representations that are useful for the final task (producing correct sentences) rather than an intermediate proxy.

## Results

Performance on the Brain-to-Text benchmarks:

| System | Architecture | WER |
|--------|-------------|-----|
| Previous SOTA (cascaded) | Phoneme decoder + 5-gram LM | ~6.5% |
| Previous SOTA (end-to-end) | Direct seq2seq | 24.69% |
| BIT (cascaded) | Pretrained encoder + n-gram LM | New SOTA |
| BIT (end-to-end) | Pretrained encoder + Aero1-Audio 1.5B | 10.22% |

The cascaded variant sets a new state-of-the-art on both Brain-to-Text '24 and '25 benchmarks. The end-to-end variant cuts the previous end-to-end WER by 58%.

The 10.22% end-to-end WER is still higher than the best cascaded systems (~6.5%), but the gap has narrowed dramatically. End-to-end systems have a structural advantage as they scale: more data and larger LLMs should improve performance continuously, while cascaded systems hit diminishing returns because each stage is independently optimized.

## Inner Speech Transfer

The most speculative and arguably most important result: BIT's pretrained encoder aligns attempted speech embeddings with imagined speech embeddings.

Attempted speech is what paralyzed participants produce when they try to speak — the motor cortex fires as if producing speech, but the signals don't reach the muscles. Imagined speech is when participants think about speaking without attempting to produce it. The neural signatures are weaker and less distinct.

BIT shows that an encoder pretrained on attempted speech data and then fine-tuned on a small amount of imagined speech data can decode imagined speech. The cross-task transfer works because the pretrained encoder already understands the temporal dynamics of speech motor planning — imagined speech activates the same networks, just at reduced amplitude.

If this finding generalizes, it opens a path toward BCIs that decode internal thought without requiring the user to attempt overt speech production. That's a different category of device — less a speech prosthetic, more a thought interface.

## Architectural Lessons

BIT validates several patterns that extend beyond BCI:

**Foundation model pretraining works for neural data.** The self-supervised recipe (mask and reconstruct) transfers from text and images to neural recordings despite the signals being orders of magnitude noisier and more variable. The key ingredient is having enough diverse data to learn general dynamics rather than subject-specific noise patterns.

**Cross-domain data improves target-domain performance.** Monkey motor data helping human speech decoding is the neural equivalent of ImageNet pretraining helping medical imaging. When the underlying dynamics share structure (neural population dynamics in motor cortex), cross-domain pretraining provides useful inductive biases even when the surface features differ substantially.

**End-to-end training beats cascaded pipelines when you have enough data and compute.** This is the same lesson as end-to-end speech recognition (replacing HMM-GMM-LM cascades with Whisper-style models) applied to neural signals. The cascade works when data is scarce because each stage can be trained independently on different data sources. End-to-end wins when you can train the whole system jointly.

**Small LLMs are sufficient.** BIT uses a 1.5B parameter LLM, not a 70B frontier model. For the constrained vocabulary and sentence structures in current BCI datasets, a small model with good neural input is more effective than a large model with poor neural input. This has practical implications for on-device deployment — a 1.5B model can run on edge hardware, making real-time BCI feasible without cloud inference.

## What's Next

The immediate bottleneck is data. 367 hours of intracranial recording is a lot by neuroscience standards — it represents years of patient sessions across multiple research sites. But by deep learning standards, it's tiny. Scaling BIT to thousands of hours of neural data, incorporating more species, more cortical areas, and more task types, should improve the pretrained encoder substantially.

The longer-term question is whether this architecture extends to non-invasive recording modalities. Utah arrays give high-quality signals because they sit inside the cortex. EEG sits outside the skull and captures signals that are smeared, attenuated, and mixed. The foundation model approach — pretrain on massive diverse data, fine-tune on the target — could potentially overcome EEG's signal quality limitations through sheer data scale. But nobody has demonstrated this yet.

The BIT paper shows that the recipe that transformed NLP and vision — foundation models, self-supervised pretraining, end-to-end training, scaling — applies to neural interfaces. Speech BCIs are following the same trajectory as speech recognition: from cascaded expert systems to end-to-end learned systems, with foundation models providing the critical pretraining step.
