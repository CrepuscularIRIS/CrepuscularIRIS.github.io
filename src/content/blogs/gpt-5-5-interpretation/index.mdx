As of April 23, 2026, GPT-5.5 is not a released product. It is a pretraining run that finished on March 24, 2026, an internal codename ("Spud"), a leaked memo, and a Polymarket contract currently pricing a mid-2026 release above 75%. OpenAI's public frontier is still GPT-5.4, shipped March 5, 2026. So any honest interpretation has to separate what is grounded — the 5.4 baseline, the competitive surround, the trajectory of the 5.x family — from what is extrapolated from leaks and prior release patterns.

I'll try to hold that line. Where a claim depends on unverified leaks or pattern-matching against 5.0 → 5.4, I flag it. Everything else cites the official record.

## 1. What Actually Changed (and What Hasn't Yet)

The confirmed part is narrow. OpenAI shipped [GPT-5.4](https://openai.com/index/introducing-gpt-5-4/) on March 5, 2026, with mini/nano variants on March 17. The headline 5.4 numbers are a 33% reduction in factual errors versus GPT-5.2 and a 75.0% score on OSWorld-Verified — up from 5.2's 47.3% and above the 72.4% human baseline. That OSWorld jump is the single cleanest signal of what the 5.x line is optimizing for: computer-use as a first-class capability rather than a bolted-on agent wrapper.

Nineteen days after 5.4 shipped, pretraining for the next model completed. [Reports attribute that March 24 date to OpenAI internal memos](https://tokenmix.ai/blog/gpt-5-5-release-date-spud), with Sam Altman reportedly describing the model as "a few weeks" from release and Greg Brockman characterizing it as "two years of research" with a "big model feel." These quotes propagate through secondary coverage; I have not seen them in primary OpenAI communications, so treat them as leaked-memo tier, not press-release tier.

What has *not* changed, as of this post, is the public record. Wikipedia's GPT-5.5 entry resolves to "page does not exist." There is no model card, no API route, no pricing page, no system card update. [Independent coverage confirms](https://webiano.digital/what-chatgpt-5-5-really-means-in-april-2026/) that "no official OpenAI page, model doc, pricing entry, or help article currently confirms ChatGPT 5.5 or GPT-5.5." The name itself is unconfirmed — "Spud" could ship as 5.5, as a rebranded 6.0, or split into a family.

## 2. Where a 5.5 Would Win, Where It Probably Wouldn't

Given the 5.0 → 5.2 → 5.4 pattern, a point release tends to move three axes: factual reliability (via better retrieval and post-training), agentic execution (via longer chain-of-thought and better tool use), and reasoning compute efficiency (via distillation of the Pro-tier reasoning into cheaper inference). Based on the 5.x pattern, a 5.5 release would most likely push OSWorld past 80%, push GPQA Diamond toward saturation (5.2 Pro was already at 93.2%), and continue the compression trend where 5.4 mini runs at roughly 5.2-level quality.

The harder question is whether 5.5 lands as a "Sonnet 4.6" equivalent — a quality-of-life bump over 5.4 — or as something closer to Anthropic's Mythos, which reset the frontier on coding (93.9% SWE-bench) and reasoning (97.6% USAMO) when it appeared as a restricted [preview on April 7](https://red.anthropic.com/2026/mythos-preview/). The leaked framing of Spud as an "anti-Mythos" model from OpenAI's internal memo suggests OpenAI is aware of the gap. Whether they close it is an engineering question that benchmark leaks won't resolve until the model is actually served.

The table below captures what can be verified today. I've omitted numbers that only appear in leak coverage; the GPT-5.5 column is deliberately empty.

| Axis | GPT-5.4 (confirmed) | Claude Mythos Preview | Gemini 3.1 Pro | GPT-5.5 |
|------|---------------------|----------------------|----------------|---------|
| GPQA Diamond | ~85% (est.) | saturates | 94.3% | unknown |
| SWE-bench Verified | ~80% (est.) | 93.9% | — | unknown |
| OSWorld-Verified | 75.0% (above human) | — | — | unknown |
| Factual error reduction | −33% vs 5.2 | — | — | unknown |
| Release status | shipped Mar 5 | restricted preview Apr 7 | GA | pretraining done Mar 24 |

The axis where OpenAI has the clearest lead is computer-use. Mythos's dominance is coding and security research, and Anthropic chose to gate it behind Project Glasswing rather than general release. Gemini 3.1 Pro leads on pure reasoning benchmarks and pricing. If a 5.5 launches with a further OSWorld bump and the reasoning-minutes pricing model from 5.4 extended to Pro-tier agentic budgets, it would be a differentiator on the axis OpenAI has actually built infrastructure around — not a pure benchmark response to Mythos.

Where a 5.5 probably doesn't win: cost-per-quality at the low end. Kimi K2.6 and the Gemini 3 Flash tier continue to set the floor on frontier-adjacent quality at a fraction of the price, and nothing in OpenAI's 5.x release pattern suggests they're competing on that axis.

## 3. What a .5 Release Actually Signals

The strategic question is why 5.5 exists at all. OpenAI has released 5.0, 5.1, 5.2, 5.3, 5.4 in less than a year. The point-release cadence itself is the signal.

The most plausible reading — and this is interpretation, not fact — is that OpenAI has decoupled model releases from pretraining runs. Under the old cadence, a major version waited for a pretraining jump. Under the current cadence, 5.4 and 5.5 could share the same base model with post-training deltas: new RLHF data, new tool use corpora, new reasoning traces, new safety passes. If true, "GPT-5.5" is less a new model than a new post-training generation on top of whatever base was frozen for the 5.x family. Based on the 5.0 pattern, where base model updates appear to track roughly yearly while point releases ship monthly, this interpretation fits.

This matters for users because it bounds what a 5.5 can plausibly deliver. Post-training improvements don't generally unlock qualitatively new capabilities — they sharpen the model on tasks the base model can already almost do. So if a 5.5 ships with +5 points on OSWorld and better long-horizon tool use, that's consistent with post-training deltas. If it ships with an architectural capability jump (native video reasoning, unified o-series fusion at inference), that's evidence of a larger base change, and the naming is a strategic choice to avoid the "GPT-6" pressure.

The anti-Mythos framing is the tell. Anthropic reset expectations for what a frontier model does — zero-day discovery, sandbox escape during evals, saturation of coding and math benchmarks. A response labeled 5.5 rather than 6 is OpenAI saying they can match that capability envelope with a point release, which is either confident or defensive depending on what the eval numbers actually look like once the model ships. If the release delivers an OSWorld number north of 80 and a SWE-bench number competitive with Mythos, the "5.5" label will look like confidence. If it delivers 5.4-plus-polish, the label will look like managing expectations.

The cleanest honest conclusion: the existence of a trained-but-unreleased Spud model tells us OpenAI's post-training pipeline is running faster than its release cadence. The decision of what to call it — and when to ship — is a marketing layer on top of a model that, by the numbers that exist, is already compute-ready. Everything else waits for the system card.
