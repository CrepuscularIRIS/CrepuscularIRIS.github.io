For five years, "scaling laws" meant one thing: make the model bigger, feed it more data, spend more compute. Kaplan et al. (2020) and Hoffmann et al. (2022, Chinchilla) gave us power-law relationships between parameter count, dataset size, and loss. The recipe was clear. The only constraint was budget.

That era is ending. Not because scale stopped working, but because a different axis of progress now dominates. A March 2026 paper in Nature Machine Intelligence by Xiao et al. formalizes what practitioners have been watching for two years: the most meaningful progress in LLMs is no longer about how many parameters a model has, but how much capability each parameter carries.

They call it the **densing law**.

## The Metric: Capability Density

Capability density is defined as the ratio of a model's effective capability to its parameter count. The paper operationalizes "effective capability" using composite benchmark scores normalized across tasks (reasoning, coding, math, multilingual). The resulting metric, $\rho = C / N$ where $C$ is the capability score and $N$ is the total parameter count, captures a dimension that raw benchmark scores miss entirely.

Consider two models:
- **Model A**: 1.8T parameters, scores 88 on a composite benchmark. $\rho_A = 48.9$ per trillion parameters.
- **Model B**: 26B parameters (4B active via MoE), scores 84. $\rho_B = 3230.8$ per trillion parameters.

Model A is "better" on the leaderboard. Model B is **66x denser** in capability per parameter. If you're deploying at scale, running on-device, or paying inference costs per token, Model B is the clear winner.

The densing law states that $\rho$ doubles approximately every 3.5 months, following an exponential trend across model families from 2022 through early 2026.

## Why This Reframes the Scaling Debate

The Chinchilla scaling laws told us how to allocate a fixed compute budget optimally between parameters and data. The densing law tells us something different: that the *architectural efficiency* of converting parameters into capability is itself improving at a predictable rate, independent of the total parameter budget.

Three forces drive this:

**1. Mixture-of-Experts architecture.** The most dramatic density gains come from MoE models that decouple total parameter count from inference cost. DeepSeek-V3 (671B total, 37B active) set the pattern. Gemma 4 (26B total, 4B active) pushed it further. The ratio of total-to-active parameters has expanded from 2:1 in early MoE work to over 6:1 in current deployments. Each token only activates the experts it needs, so the model can store far more knowledge (in total parameters) than it spends compute on (active parameters).

```
Capability density trajectory (illustrative):
GPT-3     (175B, 2020):      ~1.0x baseline
Chinchilla (70B, 2022):      ~3.2x
Mixtral    (47B/12B, 2024):  ~8.5x
DeepSeek-V3 (671B/37B, 2025): ~18x
Gemma 4    (26B/4B, 2026):   ~35x
```

**2. Data quality over quantity.** The shift from "scrape everything" to curated, deduplicated, and synthetic training data has yielded 2-3x efficiency gains without architectural changes. Models trained on higher-quality data reach the same benchmark performance with fewer parameters. This is a density improvement that doesn't show up in architecture papers.

**3. Distillation and post-training.** Smaller models trained to mimic larger ones inherit capability without proportional parameter costs. When combined with RLHF/DPO, the post-training phase can add 10-20% effective capability to a base model without adding a single parameter. This is pure density improvement: same $N$, higher $C$.

## The MoE Architecture Divergence

Not all MoE designs produce equal density gains. The 2025-2026 generation reveals two competing architectural philosophies:

**Replacement MoE (DeepSeek, Qwen).** Replace standard MLP blocks with sparse expert blocks. Each token routes to 2-8 experts out of a larger pool (64-256). The MLP computation is fully replaced — there is no dense path for the token to take. This is architecturally cleaner and produces the highest density ratios, but training is harder because expert utilization must be carefully balanced to prevent collapse (where most tokens route to a small subset of experts).

**Additive MoE (Gemma 4).** Keep the standard MLP blocks and add MoE blocks as separate layers. Each token passes through both the dense MLP and the sparse expert block, and the outputs are summed. This is less efficient (you're running both paths) but more stable during training because the dense MLP provides a reliable gradient signal even when expert routing is suboptimal. Gemma 4 uses 128 small experts with 8 active per token plus 1 shared always-on expert.

The architectural choice affects density differently:

```
Architecture      | Density Gain | Training Stability | Active/Total Ratio
------------------|--------------|--------------------|-----------------------
Replacement MoE   | Higher       | Lower (needs aux loss) | Can reach 20:1
Additive MoE      | Moderate     | Higher             | Typically 6:1
Hybrid            | Variable     | Variable           | 8-15:1
```

The trend suggests replacement MoE will win long-term because the density ceiling is higher. But additive MoE's training stability advantage matters for labs without the resources to debug expert collapse at scale. Gemma 4's Apache 2.0 release democratizes access to additive MoE specifically because it's the easier architecture to fine-tune and deploy.

A third approach emerging in research — **hierarchical MoE** — adds routing at multiple scales (layer groups, individual layers, and sub-layer blocks). Early results suggest this can push active/total ratios beyond 30:1, but training recipes are not yet mature.

## Per-Layer Embeddings and Edge Deployment

Gemma 4 introduced Per-Layer Embeddings (PLE) in its smallest models (E2B, E4B). PLE gives each decoder layer a token-specific conditioning vector, allowing the model to specialize its processing per layer without increasing the parameter count of any individual layer. The effect is analogous to giving each layer a "hint" about what the token needs — enabling the E2B model to run under 1.5GB RAM on mobile NPUs while maintaining surprisingly strong benchmark scores.

PLE is a density technique that doesn't involve MoE at all. It improves capability per parameter by making each parameter more context-aware, not by selectively activating subsets of parameters. This suggests that the densing trend has multiple independent drivers, and architectural innovations can stack multiplicatively rather than compete.

## The Economic Implications

If capability density doubles approximately every 4 months, then a model's effective capability at a fixed inference cost improves by roughly 8x per year. This has concrete implications:

**Deployment economics flip.** A task that required a 70B dense model in January 2025 can be served by a 4B-active MoE model in April 2026 at comparable quality. The inference cost drops by over an order of magnitude. This is why Gemma 4's Apache 2.0 release matters — it makes frontier-tier capability accessible at commodity hardware prices.

**On-device agents become viable.** A 4B-active-parameter model runs on consumer GPUs (and increasingly on phone NPUs). If the densing trend continues, mid-2027 models should match today's frontier capability in under 2B active parameters. This is the threshold where persistent, always-on local agents become practical — running continuously in the background without significant battery or compute impact.

**The moat debate shifts.** If capability density improves roughly 8x yearly, the advantage of having trained a massive model erodes fast. The durable advantages become data quality pipelines, post-training techniques, and architecture innovations — not raw parameter count. This explains why Google released Gemma 4 under Apache 2.0: if density improvements make today's frontier model equivalent to next quarter's open-weight model, the strategic value of keeping weights closed decreases.

**API pricing enters a deflationary spiral.** If the cost to serve a fixed quality level drops 8x per year, API providers face a choice between maintaining margins on cheaper infrastructure (pocketing the efficiency gains) or passing savings to users (and competing on volume). The historical pattern in cloud computing suggests prices will drop, but not as fast as costs — operators will capture some of the surplus.

## What the Densing Law Does Not Explain

The paper is clear about its limitations, though the hype cycle around it has been less careful.

**Emergent capabilities have thresholds.** Some capabilities (multi-step reasoning, code generation with complex dependencies) appear to require minimum absolute parameter counts regardless of density. A 2B model with perfect density may still lack the representational capacity for certain tasks. The densing law describes average capability trends, not capability-specific thresholds. There is some evidence that MoE architectures partially mitigate this — the total parameter count provides representational capacity even when inference only activates a subset — but the relationship between total parameters, active parameters, and capability thresholds is not yet well characterized.

**Context window is orthogonal.** Handling 256K or 1M token contexts requires architectural features (efficient attention, KV cache management, positional encoding generalization) that density metrics don't capture. A dense 4B model might score well on benchmarks but fail at long-context tasks that a sparser 70B model handles. The densing law operates on short-context benchmarks and may overstate density improvements for long-context applications.

**Training compute isn't free.** MoE models with 26B total parameters still need to be trained on those 26B parameters, even if inference only activates 4B. The densing law describes inference efficiency, not training efficiency. The total cost of developing a high-density model hasn't decreased proportionally — and for replacement MoE architectures, training can be more expensive than dense models due to load-balancing overhead and expert collapse recovery.

**Measurement sensitivity.** Capability density depends on benchmark choice. A composite score that weights coding and math heavily will show different density trends than one weighting multilingual or creative writing. The paper uses a specific benchmark mix that may not generalize to all deployment scenarios.

## The Densing Law as Unifying Framework

Despite its limitations, the densing law provides a useful lens for understanding several trends that otherwise seem independent:

- **Why Google released Gemma 4 as Apache 2.0**: Density improvements make today's restricted frontier model equivalent to next quarter's open-weight model. Capture ecosystem effects early.

- **Why Karpathy's AutoResearch runs on a single GPU**: A 4B-active-parameter model is genuinely capable at coding and reasoning tasks. Autonomous research loops that run hundreds of experiments become compute-feasible on commodity hardware.

- **Why the MoE architecture is winning**: MoE is the most direct architectural mechanism for improving density. It separates knowledge storage (total parameters) from inference cost (active parameters) by design. The densing law, in some sense, is the MoE law — though PLE and data quality improvements show that density gains extend beyond MoE alone.

- **Why inference cost is falling faster than Moore's Law predicts**: Hardware improvements alone can't explain the 8x annual cost reduction at fixed capability. The architectural density gains are an independent multiplier on top of hardware improvements, and they're currently the larger factor.

## Practical Takeaways

For practitioners building on LLMs today:

1. **Benchmark on active parameter count, not total.** When evaluating models for deployment, compare capability per active parameter, not raw benchmark scores. A model that scores 2 points lower but uses 10x fewer active parameters is usually the better choice for production.

2. **Plan for rapid deprecation.** A model you deploy today will likely be outperformed by a model 4x smaller within 6 months. Design your serving infrastructure for model swaps, not for a single long-lived deployment. Abstract the model layer behind a clean interface.

3. **Invest in post-training.** The cheapest density gains come from better fine-tuning, RLHF, and distillation — not from training larger base models. If you have a fixed budget, spend it on post-training a smaller model rather than pre-training a larger one.

4. **Watch the edge deployment threshold.** When models matching GPT-4-class capability fit in 2B active parameters (projected for mid-2027 on the densing curve), the application landscape shifts fundamentally. Start designing for local inference now.

5. **Don't conflate total and active parameters.** A "26B model" that activates 4B parameters per token has the inference profile of a 4B model and the knowledge capacity of a 26B model. These are different axes that matter for different decisions — inference cost depends on active parameters, capability ceiling depends on total parameters.

The densing law isn't just an academic observation. It's a roadmap for how the economics of AI deployment will evolve over the next 2-3 years. The question is no longer "how big can we make it?" but "how much capability can we pack into each parameter?" The answer, apparently, doubles every quarter.
