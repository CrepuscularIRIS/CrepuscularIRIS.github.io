Anthropic named its new top-tier model **Mythos**, and then refused to sell it to you. That two-part gesture — a new capability tier *plus* a deliberate withholding — is the most interesting frontier-lab move of 2026 so far, and it deserves a closer read than the 93.9% SWE-bench headline has been getting.

## 1. What We Know

The factual layer is unusually clean, because the model leaked before it shipped. On March 26, 2026, a CMS misconfiguration exposed a draft Anthropic blog post; Fortune ran the story the same day, quoting Anthropic calling Mythos "a step change" over their existing Opus line ([Fortune, 2026-03-26](https://fortune.com/2026/03/26/anthropic-says-testing-mythos-powerful-new-ai-model-after-data-leak-reveals-its-existence-step-change-in-capabilities/)). Anthropic then formalized the release on April 7 as **Claude Mythos Preview**, paired with an umbrella program called **Project Glasswing** ([red.anthropic.com](https://red.anthropic.com/2026/mythos-preview/), [anthropic.com/glasswing](https://www.anthropic.com/glasswing)).

The name matters. "Mythos" is not `Opus 5` or `Claude 5`. Anthropic explicitly positions it as a *new tier* above Opus 4.6 and Sonnet 4.6 — the previous top of the Claude family. Published benchmarks back the framing: SWE-bench Verified 93.9%, SWE-bench Pro 77.8%, USAMO 97.6%, GPQA Diamond 94.5%, Terminal-Bench 2.0 at 82.0% (92.1% with the 2.1 patch and a 4-hour budget), and a 1M-token input / 128K-token output context window ([llm-stats Mythos card](https://llm-stats.com/models/claude-mythos-preview)). On the head-to-head tables compiled by third parties, Mythos leads GPT-5.4 and Gemini 3.1 Pro on SWE-bench Pro (77.8 vs. 57.7 vs. 54.2) and on long-context GraphWalks BFS (80 vs. 21.4 vs. Opus's 38.7) ([officechai comparison](https://officechai.com/ai/claude-mythos-benchmarks-vs-gemini-3-1-pro-gpt-5-4/)).

The wrapper around those numbers is the unusual part. Mythos Preview is **not generally available**. Access is gated through Project Glasswing: twelve founding partners (AWS, Apple, Broadcom, Cisco, CrowdStrike, Google, JPMorgan, Linux Foundation, Microsoft, NVIDIA, Palo Alto Networks, and Anthropic itself), plus roughly forty additional critical-infrastructure organizations and open-source maintainers. Anthropic is seeding the program with $100M in model-usage credits and $4M in direct donations to OSS security groups ([anthropic.com/glasswing](https://www.anthropic.com/glasswing)). Preview pricing, for partners who do pay, is $25 / $125 per million input/output tokens — roughly 1.7x Opus-tier rates. The justification is specific and unusual: during internal testing, Mythos discovered thousands of previously-unknown vulnerabilities, including a 27-year-old OpenBSD TCP SACK bug and a 16-year-old FFmpeg flaw that automated fuzzers had missed an estimated five million times. UK AISI independently evaluated the cyber uplift ([aisi.gov.uk](https://www.aisi.gov.uk/blog/our-evaluation-of-claude-mythos-previews-cyber-capabilities)). Anthropic decided the offensive cyber delta over the public frontier was large enough that broad release would be net-negative for software security.

## 2. What Would Make It Significant

Benchmarks are easy to inflate and easy to misread. The more useful question is: which of the Claude 4.x trajectories does Mythos actually extend, and which does it break?

**The coding/agentic trajectory is extended, sharply.** Opus 4.6 was already the model you reached for when you wanted an agent to survive a long tool-use loop without losing the plot. Mythos's Terminal-Bench 2.0 jump from 65.4% to 82.0% — and 92.1% with a 4-hour timeout — is the signature of a model that has genuinely been trained for multi-hour agency, not just marginally better single-turn reasoning. If Mythos behaves in production the way the OSS-Fuzz numbers suggest (595 tier-1/2 crashes and 10 full control-flow hijacks across 1,000 repos, versus ~150-175 tier-1 crashes for Opus 4.6), the practical consequence is that an autonomous coding agent can now chain 2-4 primitives into a working exploit or fix with minimal human scaffolding. That is a capability regime, not a benchmark delta.

**The context-and-reasoning trajectory is extended, but less cleanly.** A 1M-token input window was already available from Gemini; what matters is whether the model uses it. GraphWalks BFS at 80% vs. GPT-5.4's 21.4% is suggestive — multi-hop reasoning over long structured context is where most frontier models still collapse — but it's a single benchmark from a single vendor. Plausibly, Mythos combines a wider context with improved *server-side compaction* for long agentic runs, which is the practical bottleneck most production teams hit before they hit a raw token limit. Based on the 4.x trajectory, I'd expect the real improvement to be retention and coherence over 10+ hour sessions rather than absolute context length.

**The safety trajectory is where Mythos genuinely breaks form.** Every prior Claude release followed the same template: publish the model card, publish the RSP evaluation, open the API. Mythos inverts this. The model card is published ([Bedrock Mythos model card](https://docs.aws.amazon.com/bedrock/latest/userguide/model-card-anthropic-claude-mythos-preview.html)), the evaluations are published, and then the API door is closed for everyone outside the coalition. This is the first time a frontier lab has concluded that a general-purpose model is too dangerous to sell *even to paying enterprise customers with agreements in place*. Whether you read that as principled or theatrical depends on your priors — but as a precedent, it is new.

**What would make Mythos historically significant**, if the benchmarks hold up under independent replication, is the combination of those three: a model that is meaningfully better at multi-hour autonomous work, meaningfully better at long-context reasoning, *and* deemed unsafe to release broadly. If only the first two were true, Mythos would be Opus 5 in everything but name. The withholding is what makes it a different kind of release.

## 3. Reading the Market

Anthropic's positioning signal is worth decoding carefully. Three moves, three different competitors.

**Against OpenAI, the message is "we will not race you to general availability."** GPT-5.4 shipped through the normal ChatGPT / API funnel. Mythos does not. Anthropic is betting that enterprise buyers — particularly in regulated sectors and government — will read gated release as *rigor* rather than *unavailability*. The NSA integration and the White House chief-of-staff meeting about Mythos ([SC Media](https://www.scworld.com/brief/anthropic-ceo-white-house-chief-of-staff-convene-on-claude-mythos)) suggest that bet is partly working. This is a Stratechery-style strategic substitution: trade consumer breadth for policy-level depth.

**Against Google, the message is "capability-per-deployment, not capability-per-API-call."** Gemini 3.1 Pro is on every Google surface and trails Mythos on several of the published benchmarks. But Google's distribution is its moat, and Anthropic cannot contest it directly. Instead, Anthropic is making Mythos the *reference model* for critical-infrastructure security work and letting that halo pull enterprise Opus/Sonnet traffic toward Claude Code and the Anthropic API. Plausibly, Glasswing is partly a distribution hack: the forty-plus partner organizations are exactly the accounts Anthropic wants long-term.

**Against the Chinese frontier (Moonshot's Kimi line, DeepSeek, Qwen), the message is "frontier capability now requires a trust regime you cannot easily replicate."** Open-weights labs cannot withhold a model after release, and they cannot credibly promise coordinated 90-day disclosure windows. If Mythos-class cyber capability becomes a normal feature of frontier models, the open-weights ecosystem faces a structural problem that a better base model alone cannot solve.

The risk in Anthropic's play is the obvious one: a frontier lab that keeps its best model off the market will eventually be routed around. If GPT-5.5 or Gemini 4 ships at Mythos-adjacent capability *without* Glasswing-style gating, Anthropic's move looks less like leadership and more like self-imposed handicap. Based on the 4.7 trajectory and Anthropic's stated RSP commitments, I'd expect a descendant of Mythos — "Mythos 1.x" with additional cybersecurity safeguards, per the Glasswing page — to ship more broadly within 6-9 months. The question Mythos is really asking the rest of the industry is whether that gap is defensible, or just a preview of the norm everyone else will eventually be forced to adopt.
