In mid-March 2026, China's National Medical Products Administration (NMPA) approved Neuracle Technology's NEO as the world's first Class III implantable brain-computer interface for commercial clinical use. This is not a research exemption, not an expanded access pathway, not an IDE. It is a full commercial approval — the kind that lets hospitals order the device, bill through the relevant medical procedure pathways, and treat it as part of the rehabilitation pipeline. Follow-on approvals for other Chinese implantable BCIs have been reported in the weeks since, though the formal NMPA notices for those secondary devices should be checked against primary sources before citing specific dates.

The NEO approval followed a multi-center, 32-patient trial across Chinese hospitals. Patients with spinal cord injury (SCI) showed measurable improvement in hand grasping function. The regulatory milestone deserves attention not because China "beat" the US — that framing misses the point — but because it reveals how different regulatory philosophies, different device architectures, and different target populations produce wildly different timelines for technology that is, at the pre-regulatory level, broadly similar.

This post walks through the device design, the trial data, the regulatory divergence, and the specific research questions that remain open. If you follow BCI research, you know the broad strokes. The details are where the interesting tradeoffs live.

## The Device: Minimally Invasive by Design

NEO's architecture makes deliberate engineering tradeoffs that distinguish it from Neuralink's N1 and from the higher-resolution research devices at BrainGate, BlackRock Neurotech, and Paradromics.

**Form factor.** A coin-sized wireless implant, roughly 20mm in diameter and under 2mm thick, with 8 epidural electrodes arranged in a 2×4 grid. "Epidural" is the key word: the electrodes sit on top of the dura mater, the outermost membrane covering the brain. They never penetrate brain tissue. This is a fundamentally different risk profile than intracortical arrays that insert electrodes into the cortex.

**Signal quality vs. invasiveness.** Epidural recordings sacrifice spatial resolution compared to intracortical recordings. You get field potentials from populations of neurons rather than single-unit activity. The typical signal-to-noise ratio for epidural electrocorticography (ECoG) falls between scalp EEG (worst) and penetrating arrays (best):

```
Signal quality hierarchy:
  Scalp EEG        ~10 μV amplitude, broad spatial mixing
  Epidural ECoG    ~100 μV amplitude, ~1 cm resolution    ← NEO
  Subdural ECoG    ~500 μV amplitude, ~0.5 cm resolution
  Intracortical    ~200 μV single units, ~100 μm resolution ← Neuralink N1
```

NEO compensates for lower spatial resolution by targeting a simpler decoding task — imagined hand movements for controlling a robotic glove — where population-level signals over primary motor cortex provide sufficient discriminability. The decoder is a multi-class classifier mapping ECoG features (mu-band and high-gamma power, extracted from 250ms windows) to a small alphabet of grasp commands: open, close, pinch, rest. This is a tractable classification problem, not a continuous control problem.

**Wireless power and data.** No percutaneous connectors. Power arrives via a near-field inductive link from a wearable coil that sits over the implant site; data returns via a low-bandwidth radio link to the same external controller. The wearable coil is the part the patient actually handles. The implant itself has no battery — everything runs on harvested inductive power, which caps available compute and radio budget. This is why the decoder is small: the implant cannot run a transformer, so on-device signal conditioning is followed by compressed data transmission to the external controller, which runs the classifier.

Removing the percutaneous connector matters clinically. A wire passing through the skin is a permanent breach of the body's primary infection barrier, and chronic Utah-array implants in research settings routinely develop infections at the connector site. Of patients in the BrainGate long-term studies, a meaningful fraction required connector site revision over multi-year follow-up. NEO avoids this entirely. The tradeoff is bandwidth: the implant cannot stream wideband neural data at the rates a research tether allows. For a single-DOF motor decoder, this is fine; for a speech BCI that needs 2kHz sampling across hundreds of channels, it is not.

**Chronic biocompatibility.** Epidural placement avoids the gliosis and electrode degradation that plague intracortical arrays. The tissue reaction to a device sitting on top of the dura is milder than the reaction to electrodes crossing the blood-brain barrier and displacing neurons. That said, epidural implants can still develop fibrotic encapsulation over years. The trial's longest follow-up is under 24 months. The true durability curve is an open question.

## The Trial Data

The 32-patient multi-center trial is small by pharmaceutical standards but large by BCI standards, where most published studies involve 1 to 5 participants.

**Population.** Spinal cord injury patients at the C4–C7 cervical level — a well-defined target population where the motor cortex is intact but descending pathways are disrupted. This is the cleanest use case for a motor BCI: the neural signals encoding intended movement are present and decodable; the body simply cannot execute them. It is also a population with a relatively high tolerance for implantable devices, given the severity of the underlying condition.

**Primary outcome.** Every patient showed improvement in hand grasping function when using the BCI-controlled robotic glove. The metric is functional — can the patient grasp and manipulate objects using standardized Action Research Arm Test scoring — rather than abstract decoder accuracy. This matters. Many BCI research papers report decoder classification accuracy (90%+ on offline data) without showing corresponding functional improvement. The NEO trial used functional benchmarks as the primary endpoint.

**Neural plasticity finding.** Perhaps the most scientifically interesting result: improvements in grasping function persisted after the device was temporarily removed for maintenance. This suggests the BCI training induced neural reorganization that outlasted the device itself. If confirmed in larger studies with longer follow-up, this transforms the value proposition from "permanent prosthetic" to "therapeutic intervention with lasting effects."

The persistence effect has precedent in stroke rehabilitation, where constraint-induced movement therapy forces the brain to reorganize motor representations. A BCI that provides consistent, contingent feedback on motor imagery could drive similar plasticity through Hebbian reinforcement: imagine moving the hand, receive immediate visual and tactile feedback of movement, strengthen the relevant cortical representations. Over months of daily use, the cortex may learn to drive any spared descending pathways more effectively. Whether the plasticity is genuinely durable across years, and whether it depends on the specific BCI protocol or transfers to other motor tasks, are questions the trial cannot answer.

**Safety outcomes.** No serious adverse events across 32 patients over the reported follow-up. Minor events included local skin irritation at the inductive coil site and one case of transient post-operative headache. Critically, zero infections and zero implant revisions. For a cohort this size over this duration, the safety signal is encouraging but underpowered for rare events. A 32-patient trial has roughly a 3% chance of observing even one case of a complication with 0.1% incidence. Populations of thousands are needed to characterize the long-tail safety profile.

## The Regulatory Divergence

NEO's approval path illustrates a structural difference between NMPA, FDA, and EMA approaches to novel medical devices.

**FDA's current position on implantable BCIs.** Neuralink received a Breakthrough Device Designation in 2023 and began its PRIME study (N1 implant) in January 2024. As of April 2026, N1 remains in clinical trials. The FDA pathway requires: IDE approval → pivotal trial → PMA submission → review → approval. Each stage has defined evidence requirements and review timelines.

Synchron's Stentrode (endovascular BCI) has been in the FDA pipeline since 2021 and is further along, but also not yet commercially approved. Precision Neuroscience's Layer 7 array received FDA 510(k) clearance for short-term intraoperative use in 2025, but not for chronic implantation. CorTec's Brain Interchange, a bidirectional BCI from Germany, received FDA Breakthrough Device Designation in April 2026 and is beginning initial implantations; commercial approval is years away.

**EU MDR.** The European Medical Device Regulation, fully in force since 2021, places Class III active implantable devices at the highest scrutiny level. Post-Brexit UK devices face parallel UKCA requirements. No implantable BCI has received CE marking under MDR as of April 2026. Even if a device had FDA approval, reaching the European market would require a separate conformity assessment.

**NMPA's approach.** China's pathway compressed the timeline through several mechanisms: smaller required trial sizes (32 patients vs. the hundreds typically expected for an FDA PMA for a Class III novel device), a national strategic priority designation for BCI technology (part of China's 2026–2030 neurotechnology development plan), and a regulatory framework that was designed with neural devices in mind rather than adapted from existing categories. The Special Approval Procedure for Innovative Medical Devices, used for NEO, explicitly prioritizes devices where the manufacturer has core technical patents and clinically significant advantages.

**The tradeoff is real.** Faster approval with smaller trials means less statistical power to detect rare adverse events. The FDA's larger trial requirements exist specifically to catch low-frequency safety signals, and the rare-event detection math is unforgiving. Neither approach is unambiguously correct; they represent different positions on the speed-safety Pareto frontier. The FDA errs toward safety at the cost of speed; the NMPA errs toward speed at the cost of small-number safety precision. Whether that tradeoff holds up depends on the post-market surveillance regime — NMPA requires mandatory real-world evidence collection for devices approved under the innovative pathway, which partially mitigates the smaller pre-approval population.

## Technical Comparison: NEO vs. Neuralink N1

| Dimension | NEO | Neuralink N1 |
|-----------|-----|--------------|
| Electrode placement | Epidural (on dura) | Intracortical (in cortex) |
| Electrode count | 8 | 1,024 |
| Surgical invasiveness | Minimally invasive craniotomy | Robotic microelectrode insertion |
| Infection risk profile | Low (no percutaneous) | Low (sealed implant, but tissue breaching) |
| Signal bandwidth | Population-level ECoG | Single-unit + LFP |
| On-device compute | Low (inductive-powered) | Moderate (battery-powered) |
| Target application | Motor rehabilitation | General-purpose neural interface |
| Current indication | Hand grasping in SCI | Cursor control in ALS |
| Regulatory status | Commercially approved (NMPA) | Clinical trial (FDA IDE) |
| Decoder complexity | Discrete classifier | Continuous control (2-DOF cursor) |
| Trial size at approval | 32 | n/a (still in trial) |

The comparison is not "which is better" but "which tradeoffs matter for which application." NEO optimizes for safety, deployability, and a specific clinical population. N1 optimizes for signal richness and generality at the cost of higher surgical risk and regulatory complexity. Reduce N1 to 8 channels of ECoG and it could probably clear a similar regulatory bar; reduce NEO to 8 electrodes of intracortical recording and it loses all its approval advantages.

A useful way to think about this: BCI devices occupy a four-dimensional tradeoff space — invasiveness, signal resolution, compute, and target application. Different companies make different choices along each axis. NEO's choices are sensible for motor rehabilitation. N1's choices are sensible for demanding a high-bandwidth general interface. Neither is wrong; the pairing of design choices to application is what matters.

## What This Means for the Field

**The rehabilitation pathway is the fastest route to commercial BCI.** NEO's approval validates what many BCI researchers have argued: motor rehabilitation for spinal cord injury is the most tractable clinical application because the neural signals are well-characterized, the patient population is well-defined, and the outcome measures are concrete. Starting here and expanding indications later is the pragmatic path. Expect follow-on approvals in stroke motor rehab, ALS, and eventually locked-in syndrome.

**Epidural is good enough for many applications.** The BCI field has historically chased higher signal resolution, assuming that more electrodes and deeper placement produce better outcomes. NEO's results suggest that for specific, constrained decoding tasks, epidural recordings with a small electrode count provide sufficient information. This matters because epidural implantation has dramatically lower surgical risk, which changes the risk-benefit calculation for patients. The minimum viable BCI is much smaller than the "1,024-channel intracortical" vision that dominated neurotech fundraising decks in 2023–2024.

**The plasticity finding could reshape the business model.** If BCI-induced neural plasticity reliably persists after device removal, the device becomes a temporary therapeutic intervention rather than a permanent implant. This changes reimbursement strategy (procedure vs. device), patient acceptance (temporary vs. lifelong implant), and regulatory requirements (short-term vs. chronic implant safety testing). The ceiling on BCI economics has historically been "lifetime implant with periodic revision"; the floor becomes "a six-month training course followed by device removal." Both can coexist.

**Regulatory arbitrage is now a factor.** A device approved in China for commercial use but still in clinical trials in the US and EU creates pressure on Western regulators. Medical tourism for NEO implantation is a predictable next step — SCI patients willing to travel will do so. Whether this pressure improves or degrades patient safety depends on how regulators respond: accelerating FDA and EMA timelines with preserved safety scrutiny is the good outcome; pressuring agencies to lower evidence standards is the bad one. The difference is subtle and politically contested.

**The BCI industry structure is bifurcating.** On one side: minimally invasive, single-indication, rapidly-deployable devices like NEO and Synchron's Stentrode. On the other: high-resolution, general-purpose platforms like Neuralink's N1 and Paradromics' Connexus. Each track will serve different patient populations and follow different regulatory paths. Expecting one architecture to dominate misreads the diversity of clinical need.

## The Research Questions That Remain

The 32-patient trial answers the feasibility question but leaves several critical questions open:

1. **Durability of the plasticity effect.** How long do improvements persist after device removal? The trial data shows short-term persistence over weeks; months-to-years follow-up is needed to know whether this is genuinely durable rehabilitation or a temporary facilitation effect.

2. **Decoder generalization.** NEO decodes imagined hand movements. Can the same epidural platform, with more electrodes and better decoders, extend to arm movements, speech, or other modalities? Early Chinese research groups have demonstrated epidural speech decoding in animal models, but clinical translation is uncertain.

3. **Long-term biocompatibility.** Epidural placement avoids the gliosis and electrode degradation that plague intracortical arrays, but chronic epidural implants can still develop fibrotic encapsulation that degrades signal quality over years. Five- and ten-year follow-up is essential.

4. **Decoder adaptation.** Neural signals drift over time as the brain reorganizes. Does NEO's decoder adapt to these changes online, or does it require periodic recalibration? The device's compute constraints favor offline recalibration on the external controller, but the user experience of recalibration matters for adoption.

5. **Population expansion.** Spinal cord injury is the lowest-hanging fruit. Stroke, ALS, and locked-in syndrome present different neural signal characteristics, motor cortex integrity, and decoder challenges. Expanding the indicated population will require indication-specific trials.

6. **Comparative effectiveness.** How does BCI-mediated rehabilitation compare to conventional physical therapy? BCI-guided therapy has to demonstrate not just improvement over baseline but incremental benefit over existing standards of care. This is the question insurance reimbursement will ultimately turn on.

The approval of NEO does not mean the BCI problem is solved. It means one specific configuration — epidural ECoG, motor imagery decoding, robotic glove actuation, SCI population — has crossed the threshold from research to clinical practice. The distance from this point to a general-purpose brain-computer interface remains vast. But every field advances through specific milestones, and this is a genuine one.
