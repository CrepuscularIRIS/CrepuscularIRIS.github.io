OpenAI announced in early April 2026 that Sora, its text-to-video generation platform, will shut down on April 26. The API follows in September. The stated reason is "unsustainable operational costs," with estimates ranging from $1M to $15M per day in inference compute.

This is a rare case of a frontier AI lab shutting down a high-profile product not because it failed technically, but because the economics of serving it at scale don't work. The decision reveals something important about which generative AI modalities can support commercial products and which are research demonstrations masquerading as products.

## The Arithmetic of Video Generation

To understand why Sora's costs are unsustainable, compare the compute profiles of different generative modalities at inference time.

**Text generation** (GPT-5.x, Claude Opus 4.6): The model processes a sequence of tokens autoregressively. Each token requires one forward pass through the network. A 4,000-token response on a 200B-parameter dense model requires roughly 4,000 forward passes, each involving ~200B multiply-accumulate operations. Total: ~800 trillion operations. On an H100 at ~1 PFLOP/s effective throughput, that's under a second of GPU time. At $2/GPU-hour, the raw compute cost is fractions of a cent per response.

**Image generation** (DALL-E 3, Midjourney): A diffusion model runs 20-50 denoising steps, each involving a full forward pass through a U-Net or DiT backbone at the image resolution. For a 1024x1024 image with 30 denoising steps on a 3B-parameter model, you're looking at ~30 forward passes, each over a spatial tensor that's orders of magnitude larger than a text token sequence. Total compute: roughly 100x-500x a single text generation. Cost per image: a few cents. Expensive, but serviceable with a $20/month subscription.

**Video generation** (Sora): Here the arithmetic breaks. A 10-second video at 24fps is 240 frames. Each frame has roughly the spatial complexity of an image. The temporal consistency requirement means you can't just generate 240 independent images — the model needs to maintain coherent motion, lighting, and physics across the entire sequence. Sora uses a spatiotemporal DiT architecture that processes the full video as a 3D tensor.

The naive compute estimate: take the image generation cost and multiply by frame count, then add significant overhead for temporal attention. Whether Sora uses full 3D attention or a factored spatiotemporal approach affects the exact multiplier, but even with factored attention, a single 10-second video generation likely costs 1,000x-10,000x a text response — and potentially much more with full spatiotemporal attention.

```
Modality         | Relative Compute/Request | Cost/Request (est.)
-----------------|--------------------------|--------------------
Text (4K tokens) |           1x             |    $0.001-0.01
Image (1024x1024)|        100-500x          |    $0.02-0.05
Video (10s, 24fps)|     1,000-10,000x+      |    $1-50
```

These are rough estimates — actual costs depend on architecture details, batch sizes, and hardware utilization that OpenAI hasn't disclosed. But the order of magnitude gap is what matters. Even at $1 per generation with millions of daily users, the burn rate reaches seven figures fast.

## Why Pricing Can't Fix This

The obvious objection: just charge more. If video generation costs $5-50 per clip, charge $100/month for a limited plan. The problem is demand elasticity.

Text generation has near-universal utility. Everyone writes emails, documents, code. At $20/month, hundreds of millions of people find daily value. Image generation has broad creative utility — marketing teams, designers, social media content. The willingness to pay is real but the market is smaller.

Video generation occupies an awkward niche. Professional video production has high willingness to pay, but professionals need specific, controllable outputs — not stochastic generations that might or might not match their vision. Consumer video generation (making funny clips, social media content) has high volume but near-zero willingness to pay significant amounts. The middle ground — businesses that need occasional video content but can't afford a production team — exists but isn't large enough to subsidize the compute costs at Sora's scale.

Adobe's approach with Firefly Video illustrates the alternative: integrate video generation into existing paid creative tools (Premiere Pro, After Effects) where the incremental compute cost is absorbed into an already-high subscription. OpenAI, as a pure-play AI lab, doesn't have that embedding advantage.

## What the Sora Shutdown Tells Us About AI Business Models

**Lesson 1: Inference cost is the moat, not model quality.** Sora was arguably the best text-to-video system available. Quality didn't matter — the unit economics were negative. The winning generative AI products will be those where inference cost per unit of user value is lowest, which structurally favors text and code over rich media.

**Lesson 2: The "ChatGPT for X" model has limits.** ChatGPT works because text generation is cheap enough to serve at scale with a flat subscription. Extending that model to expensive modalities creates a subsidy problem: heavy users consume orders of magnitude more compute than light users, and the subscription price can't differentiate enough to prevent adverse selection.

**Lesson 3: OpenAI is pivoting to code.** The simultaneous expansion of Codex (autonomous coding agent) and shutdown of Sora is not coincidental. Code generation has the best unit economics of any AI modality: the output (working code) has high and measurable economic value, the inference cost is comparable to text, and the evaluation is automated (does the code pass tests?). Code is where the revenue-to-compute ratio is highest.

## The Deeper Pattern: Modality Economics

The generative AI industry is sorting itself along a compute-economics spectrum that was predictable from first principles:

**Commercially viable at scale:**
- Text generation/chat (low compute, universal utility)
- Code generation (low compute, high economic value per output)
- Text-to-speech (moderate compute, broad utility)
- Image generation (moderate compute, sufficient pricing tolerance)

**Viable as features, not products:**
- Video generation (high compute, narrow pricing tolerance)
- 3D model generation (high compute, professional niche)
- Music generation (moderate compute, licensing complications)

**Research demonstrations:**
- Real-time video generation (extreme compute, no clear business model)
- World simulation (extreme compute, unclear utility)

This sorting has implications for where AI labs invest. Anthropic has largely avoided media generation, focusing on text and code — a strategic choice that looks increasingly sound. Google has the infrastructure to subsidize video generation as a loss leader for cloud services, but even they've been cautious about making Veo a standalone product.

## The Hardware Question

There's an argument that Sora's economics will improve with better hardware. Next-generation inference accelerators (GB200, TPU v6) offer 2-4x better performance per dollar. Custom ASICs for diffusion models could push further. But the gap between text and video inference costs is not 2-4x — it's 100,000x or more. Hardware improvements help at the margin but don't close a five-order-of-magnitude gap.

The more promising direction is architectural: can you generate video without the full spatiotemporal diffusion process? Approaches like frame interpolation (generate keyframes, interpolate between them), latent space video generation (work in a compressed representation), or hybrid text-to-storyboard-to-video pipelines reduce compute by sacrificing some quality. But these trade-offs move the product from "magical" to "useful tool," which changes the market positioning entirely.

## What This Means for Researchers

If you're building systems that depend on generative video, the Sora shutdown is a data point about platform risk. More broadly, it's a signal to evaluate any AI capability through the lens of inference economics before building products on top of it.

The question to ask about any AI capability: **at the usage volume required for the business model to work, what is the per-request compute cost, and does the unit economics support it?** Sora's answer was no. For text and code, the answer is comfortably yes. For everything in between, the answer depends on how you structure the product.

The most interesting near-term work in video generation isn't making better models — it's making cheaper architectures. Whoever solves the inference cost problem for video, not the quality problem, will own the market.
