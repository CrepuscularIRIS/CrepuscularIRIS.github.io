On April 6, 2026, OpenAI published "Industrial Policy for the Intelligence Age" — a 13-page document proposing specific economic policies to manage the societal impact of advanced AI. The proposals include a robot tax on automated labor displacement, a public wealth fund capitalized by AI company contributions, government-incentivized four-day workweeks, and federal retraining programs.

This is not a blog post or a position statement. It's a detailed policy blueprint with economic projections, legislative mechanisms, and implementation timelines. It's also the most explicit example to date of a frontier AI lab stepping out of the technology lane and into economic governance — and the conflicts of interest embedded in that move are worth examining carefully.

## The Proposals

**Robot tax:** Companies that automate jobs previously held by humans would pay a per-position displacement tax, calibrated to the average compensation of the displaced role. The revenue funds retraining and transition programs. OpenAI proposes a graduated implementation: voluntary reporting for two years, mandatory compliance after that.

The economic logic is straightforward. If automation generates surplus value by replacing labor with capital, some of that surplus should fund the transition costs that the displaced workers bear. This isn't a new idea — Bill Gates proposed a robot tax in 2017, and South Korea briefly implemented an automation tax credit reduction. What's new is an AI company proposing the mechanism, not opposing it.

**Public wealth fund:** A sovereign-style fund, capitalized by equity contributions from AI companies that exceed a revenue threshold. The fund distributes dividends to citizens, creating a partial basic income mechanism. OpenAI suggests AI companies contribute 2-5% of equity above a $50B valuation to the fund.

This proposal has obvious self-serving elements. OpenAI, valued at roughly $300B as of early 2026, would be one of the largest contributors — but it would also be shaping the rules of contribution. A 3% equity contribution at $300B valuation is $9B, which sounds significant until you consider that the alternative policy landscape might include windfall profit taxes, forced licensing of foundational models, or antitrust breakups. The wealth fund is expensive but predictable, which is exactly what a company planning decades-long capital allocation would prefer.

**Four-day workweek incentives:** Federal tax credits for employers who transition to a four-day workweek without reducing compensation, on the theory that AI-augmented productivity should translate to reduced working hours rather than increased output expectations.

This is the most socially appealing proposal and the least economically substantive. Companies already have the ability to implement four-day workweeks. The ones that don't are constrained by competitive dynamics (if your competitors work five days, working four is a disadvantage) or by the nature of the work (service industries, healthcare). A tax credit doesn't change these constraints meaningfully.

## The Conflict of Interest Problem

The fundamental tension: OpenAI is the entity most likely to cause the disruptions these policies are designed to mitigate. When the company building AGI writes the rulebook for managing AGI's economic impact, every proposal needs to be evaluated through the lens of "does this policy constrain OpenAI, or does it constrain OpenAI's competitors more?"

**The robot tax creates a barrier to entry for smaller AI companies.** A startup automating three jobs can't absorb the compliance overhead that a trillion-dollar company can. The per-position reporting, verification, and tax payment infrastructure favors companies with existing legal and accounting departments. OpenAI would pay more in absolute terms but less as a percentage of revenue, and the compliance burden would disproportionately affect competitors.

**The wealth fund locks in the current market structure.** Equity contributions from companies above $50B valuation means the fund is capitalized primarily by the existing frontier labs — OpenAI, Google, Anthropic, and Meta. These companies effectively buy regulatory stability: in exchange for equity contributions, they gain implicit protection from more disruptive regulatory interventions. New entrants face a dual burden: compete with well-resourced incumbents while also contributing to a fund designed by those incumbents.

**The policy document normalizes industry-led regulation.** By being the first to propose comprehensive policy, OpenAI positions itself as a responsible actor that should be consulted on regulation rather than subjected to it. This is a well-documented pattern in industry lobbying — not regulatory capture in the Stiglerian sense (where agencies serve incumbents), but a preemptive framing exercise that shapes the Overton window before legislators act.

## What's Missing from the Proposal

**No mention of open-source AI.** The entire framework assumes AI is produced by large companies and consumed by everyone else. Open-weight models (Llama, Gemma, Mistral) don't fit the revenue-based contribution model. If Meta releases Llama 4 as open-weight and it displaces jobs, who pays the robot tax? The person who deployed it? The company that trained it? The framework is silent on this, which effectively penalizes closed-source commercial AI while leaving open-source deployment unregulated.

**No international coordination mechanism.** A US-only robot tax creates an arbitrage opportunity: companies automate operations in jurisdictions without the tax and export the outputs. The document acknowledges this in a footnote but proposes no solution beyond "work with allies." In practice, unilateral automation taxes would accelerate offshoring of AI-automated work to countries without equivalent policies.

**No compute governance.** The policies address economic outputs (jobs displaced, revenue generated) but not inputs (compute consumed, energy used, data acquired). Compute governance — caps on training runs, reporting requirements for large-scale inference, energy consumption standards — would be harder for AI labs to support because it directly constrains their operations. Its absence is telling.

**No accountability for prediction accuracy.** The policy projections ("AI will automate X% of tasks by 20YY") are presented without confidence intervals, methodological transparency, or accountability mechanisms. If the projections are wrong — if AI automates fewer jobs than expected, or automates different ones — the policies become misallocated interventions. No revision triggers are specified.

## The Regulatory Landscape Comparison

OpenAI's proposal lands in a moment of global regulatory divergence:

**EU AI Act + Omnibus (April 2026):** Risk-based regulation with hard enforcement deadlines. The EU approach focuses on what AI systems *do* (classify them by risk level) rather than who benefits economically. Nudification deepfake bans and August 2026 enforcement dates reflect a content-harm focus.

**California Executive Order N-5-26 (March 2026):** State-level AI certification requirements, diverging from federal preemption attempts. California's approach creates compliance fragmentation — a company serving US users may face 50 different state-level AI regulations if the California model spreads.

**China CAC regulations (April 2026):** Content-morality focused, including bans on AI-generated intimate relationships with minors and "digital human" regulations. China's approach treats AI as a media regulation problem.

OpenAI's proposal represents a fourth model: economic-impact regulation designed by industry. Each approach reflects different assumptions about what AI risk primarily consists of. The EU sees application-level harm. California sees certification gaps. China sees content control. OpenAI sees labor displacement — which happens to be the framing that positions AI labs as the essential partners in the solution rather than the source of the problem.

## The Deeper Question

Should AI companies write industrial policy at all?

The argument for: they have the deepest technical understanding of the capability trajectory. Policy written without technical input tends to be either toothless (regulating yesterday's technology) or counterproductive (banning capabilities that are prerequisites for beneficial applications). OpenAI's proposals, whatever their self-serving elements, are at least technically coherent.

The argument against: no industry in history has successfully self-regulated against its own economic interests. Oil companies didn't write effective climate policy. Tobacco companies didn't write effective public health policy. Financial institutions didn't write effective banking regulation. The pattern is consistent: industry-designed regulation optimizes for the industry's survival, not for the public interest.

The most likely outcome is that OpenAI's document becomes one input among many in a legislative process that produces something different from what any single stakeholder proposed. The risk is that it becomes the *default* input — the Overton window anchor from which all policy discussion proceeds — because no other stakeholder has invested equivalent resources in developing a comprehensive alternative.

For researchers and engineers: the policy landscape affects your work whether you engage with it or not. The specific mechanisms — who pays the robot tax, what counts as automation, how the wealth fund is governed — will shape which AI applications are commercially viable, which are regulated into niche markets, and which are effectively banned. Understanding the economic logic of these proposals is part of understanding where the field is going.

## What Would Good Policy Look Like?

A policy framework designed for the public interest rather than industry convenience would include:

**Compute reporting requirements** — transparency on training runs, inference infrastructure, and energy consumption, independent of the model's commercial application.

**Sector-specific impact assessments** — required before deploying AI systems that affect more than a threshold number of workers, conducted by independent auditors rather than the deploying company.

**Open-source carve-outs** — policies that distinguish between commercial deployment and open research, preventing regulation from becoming a de facto barrier to academic AI research.

**International coordination mechanisms** — binding agreements, not aspirational "work with allies" statements, that prevent regulatory arbitrage.

**Sunset and revision clauses** — automatic policy review triggered by deviation between projected and actual automation rates.

None of these appear in OpenAI's proposal, which tells you something about whose interests the proposal optimizes for.
